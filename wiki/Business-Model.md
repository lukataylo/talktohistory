# Business Model

NearPast operates at the intersection of three growing markets: **AI companions, edtech, and location-based tourism**. The business is designed to layer multiple revenue streams on a single codebase, with B2B institutional licensing providing a high-margin foundation while consumer subscriptions scale.

---

## Revenue streams

### 1. Consumer subscription — NearPast Plus

| Tier | Price | What you get |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Free | £0 | 3 figure unlocks per day, 1 voice, standard tours |
| **NearPast Plus** | **£7.99/mo or £59.99/yr** | Unlimited unlocks, all voices, offline tour maps, premium character packs, ad-free |

**Why it converts:** the free tier is deliberately enjoyable but metered. Users hit the 3-unlock limit within a single walk and encounter the paywall at peak engagement — right after their first voice conversation with a historical figure. The in-context upgrade prompt converts at meaningfully higher rates than cold-traffic SaaS upsells.

---

### 2. B2B licensing — tourism boards, museums, heritage organisations

White-label licensing of the NearPast platform to institutional partners:

| Partner type | Example | Deal structure |
|-------------|---------|---------------|
| City tourism board | Visit London, VisitBritain | Annual licence (£30k–£150k/yr) for a branded app covering a city or region |
| Museum / attraction | British Museum, Tower of London | Per-app deal to extend the museum experience into the surrounding streets |
| Heritage body | Historic England, National Trust | Multi-site licence covering dozens of properties |
| Event / festival | Edinburgh Festival, Glastonbury | Short-term activation licence with event-specific figures and tours |

**Sales motion:** a single live demo at [nearpast.com](https://nearpast.com) is the pitch deck. Tourism organisations already budget for audio guides (£10–£25/visitor) and digital engagement. NearPast replaces a costly, hardware-dependent audio guide with a free-to-install app while adding AI conversation and collectibles that audio guides cannot offer.

---

### 3. Sponsored partner stops

Tours route users past cafés, bookshops, independent shops, and attractions. Partners pay to be featured:

| Format | Price (indicative) | Attribution |
|--------|--------------------|-------------|
| Standard listing | £150/month | Name + description in tour stop card |
| Featured stop | £500/month | Priority placement + custom prompt ("Ada Lovelace's favourite tea room") |
| GPS dwell attribution | included | Partner receives aggregate report: X users visited, average dwell Y minutes |

**Why this works:** users are already walking past these venues with their phones out. The tour stop is not an ad — it is helpful context. Click-to-booking conversion is measurable and reportable, making NearPast a performance marketing channel (not a display ad) for local businesses.

## Why NearPast is monetizable

**1. Location pre-qualifies intent.** A user who walks 40 metres to unlock a figure has demonstrated real engagement. Conversion rates for metered products are consistently higher when the user is already inside the experience.

**2. Session quality over session quantity.** A 15-minute voice conversation at a London landmark is a high-value session by any advertiser or partner metric. Dwell data is GPS-verified and attributable.

**3. B2B provides revenue certainty.** Consumer subscription is high-upside and long-tail. A single B2B licensing deal with a regional tourism board covers months of runway. The two models complement rather than compete.

**4. AI marginal cost is low and falling.** At the current stack (Gemini Flash + ElevenLabs Flash + gpt-4o-mini), per-unlock AI cost is approximately £0.05 at worst case. At £7.99/month subscriptions this is a sustainable unit economics story from early scale.

**5. Geographic data moat.** Curated, historically-verified figure coordinates tied to real addresses are difficult to replicate at quality. Each new city launch is a defensible content investment.

## Comparable companies

### Replika

Replika (Luka Inc., San Francisco, founded 2017) is an AI companion app — a chatbot users interact with as a friend, romantic partner, or emotional support figure. Key facts:

- **Scale:** 30+ million registered users; reportedly 500,000–1 million paying subscribers
- **Revenue:** estimated £150–300 M lifetime revenue; Replika Pro priced at ~$70/yr
- **Why it makes money:**
  - Emotional attachment creates extraordinary retention — daily active users log 20+ minutes per session
  - The paywall gates relationship personas and voice calls — the features users most want after forming attachment
  - Pure subscription with no advertising keeps the experience intimate and conversion high
- **Ceiling:** Replika operates entirely in a digital space. It cannot unlock B2B institutional revenue, tourism commerce, or partner-stop placement. Its retention is strong but it faces regulatory scrutiny around emotional dependency and safety, particularly for minors.

### Character.AI

Character.AI (c.ai, founded 2021 by ex-Google Brain researchers) lets users create and chat with AI personas — celebrities, fictional characters, and user-generated bots. Key facts:

- **Scale:** reported 20 million daily active users (2024); average session time reportedly exceeds YouTube and TikTok among teen-skewing demographic
- **Revenue:** reported $200 M+ ARR on a path toward ~$5 B valuation (2024 reporting)
- **Why it makes money:**
  - Massive engagement flywheel: high DAU/MAU ratio, very long average session time
  - c.ai+ subscription (~$10/mo) offers faster responses and exclusive features
  - Creator-bot marketplace drives viral user acquisition at near-zero cost
  - Scale effects: user-generated characters mean content supply grows without editorial cost
- **Ceiling:** content quality and safety are harder to control at scale. The teen-heavy demographic creates regulatory exposure. Revenue depends almost entirely on consumer subscriptions — no institutional B2B, no physical-world commerce, no tourism layer.

### How NearPast differs — and wins

| Dimension | Replika / Character.AI | NearPast |
|-----------|------------------------|----------|
| **Setting** | Anywhere — living room, commute, bed | Real, specific addresses — the figure's actual front door |
| **Revenue model** | Consumer subscription only | Consumer + B2B licensing + partner-stop commerce + IAP packs |
| **Addressable market** | Global consumer (social/emotional) | Consumer tourism + institutional (tourism boards, museums, heritage) |
| **Brand positioning** | Entertainment / companionship | Education, heritage, civic tourism — trusted by institutions |
| **Churn dynamics** | Emotional novelty fades; relationship persona paywall helps | Physical travel intent drives recurring use; new cities add content |
| **Regulatory surface** | Mental-health, minor-safety scrutiny | Family-friendly, educational; aligns with government tourism spending mandates |
| **Content moat** | User-generated (low quality bar) | Editorially curated, historically verified, geographically locked |
| **B2B path** | None | Immediate — tourism boards already budget for audio guides |

**The key insight:** Replika and Character.AI prove the core mechanic — deep AI conversation at the right moment drives extraordinary session length and subscription conversion. NearPast takes that proven mechanic and adds the one ingredient a phone cannot replicate: **you have to be there**. The physical constraint is a feature, not a limitation. It is what unlocks institutional contracts, measurable partner-stop ROI, and the kind of memory that makes users return every time they visit a new city.
